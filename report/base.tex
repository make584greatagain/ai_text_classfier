\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) now generate text that is virtually indistinguishable from human prose, complicating authenticity checks in journalism, academia, and social media. We benchmark classical algorithms (Multinomial Naïve Bayes, Logistic Regression), gradient-boosting methods (CatBoost, LightGBM), and a fine-tuned Transformer (BERT) on the public Detect AI-Generated Text corpus. After rigorous preprocessing and hyper-parameter search, CatBoost delivers the top accuracy of \textbf{\{ACC\_test\}}\% and a ROC-AUC of \textbf{\{AUC\_test\}}. When evaluated on an external, topic-balanced set, accuracy drops by \textbf{\{ΔACC\_ext\}}\%, exposing a domain-adaptation gap. Error analysis reveals that all models detect AI-generated passages more readily than human-written ones, suggesting feature-level bias. We discuss dataset expansion, ensemble calibration, and explainable-AI tooling as practical next steps toward robust, transparent detectors.
\end{abstract}
